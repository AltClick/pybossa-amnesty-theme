Glossary of Terms

A


abduction

The forceful and unlawful seizure and detention of someone by government agents or armed groups. See: enforced disappearance


administrative detention

Detention by order of an executive authority rather than a court or judge, often with no intention of charging or bringing the detainee to trial.
Amnesty International calls for any person in administrative detention to be charged or released, and for an end to all such systems of detention.


arbitrary detention

The UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention uses the term arbitrary detention to describe any of the following three situations:

  1. Where there is no legal basis for detention. This includes people held without charge or trial or despite a judicial order for their release, or those still in prison after the expiry of their sentence.
  2. An arrest or detention that is legal under national law but arbitrary under international standards, for example, if the relevant national law is vague, excessively broad, or violates human rights such as the right to freedom of expression.
  3. When there has been a grave violation of the detainee’s right to a fair trial.

asylum-seeker

An asylum-seeker is someone who has left their country in search of international protection, but is yet to be recognized as a refugee.

See: non-refoulement; refoulement; refugee


C


capital punishment

See: death penalty


clemency

An act, usually by the executive, showing mercy or leniency by lessening punishment (known as commuting a sentence), or even ending further punishment (that is, issuing a pardon). Clemency is used as a general term covering both commutations and pardons.

See: commute


commute

Commute means to replace a punishment, usually a death sentence, with a less severe one. For example, Amnesty called for the USA to commute to time served the 35-year jail sentence of whistleblower Chelsea Manning, who had been arrested after leaking classified government material.


conscientious objection

Refusal to perform military service on the grounds of conscience or profound personal conviction. This can include refusal to participate in a particular war (selective objection); it does not require that a person be opposed to taking part in all wars. Generally, anyone imprisoned for exercising this right will be considered by Amnesty International to be a prisoner of conscience, provided they have not rejected an alternative non-military service that is not punitive or discriminatory in length or nature.

See: prisoner of conscience


corporal punishment

Physical punishment – punishment that is inflicted on the body – imposed by judicial order or as an administrative sanction. It includes amputation, branding, caning, flogging and whipping. Corporal punishment always constitutes cruel, inhuman or degrading punishment and, in some cases, amounts to torture.

See: cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment; torture


crimes against humanity

When certain acts – including murder, torture and slavery – form part of a widespread attack on a civilian population by a state or organization. Unlike war crimes, they can take place at times of peace as well as during conflict. The Rwandan genocide of 1994 is one example of a crime against humanity.

See: genocide; war crimes


cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment

Torture and other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment should not be seen as separate categories. They are all equally prohibited under international law.

When referring to the whole range of prohibited treatment, use initially the term torture and other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.

See: torture


D


death in custody

Death in custody is used to describe deaths in prisons, other official or unofficial places of detention, or in hospitals or other situations where detainees are in the custody of law enforcement officials or military or security personnel.


death penalty

State-imposed death as punishment for a crime, following a judicial process in a recognized court.

Amnesty International opposes the death penalty in all cases, without exception, as a violation of the right to life and as the ultimate cruel, inhuman or degrading punishment.

See: cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment


death row

Death row is a particular part of a prison where prisoners under sentence of death are held.


deportation, expulsion

If a person (or a group of people) is being lawfully removed from a country, after a decision reached with adequate procedural safeguards, use the term deportation.

If the removal is unlawful, use the term expulsion. For a group, use collective expulsion or mass expulsion.

In accordance with international humanitarian law, Amnesty International opposes the deportation of people from territories under military occupation in all cases.


detention without charge/trial

See: administrative detention


disappearances

See: enforced disappearances


discrimination

Discrimination occurs when a person is unable to enjoy his or her human rights or other legal rights on an equal basis with others because of an unjustified distinction made in policy, law, or treatment based on any of the prohibited grounds. The prohibited grounds include: race, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or social origin, property, birth or other status (International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights).


displaced people

Mixed populations of internally displaced people and refugees may be described as displaced. For example, the number of displaced Iraqis is 4.2 million, of whom 2 million are refugees and 2.2 million are internally displaced.

See: asylum-seekers; internally displaced people; refugees


E


economic, social and cultural rights

A broad category of human rights including the right to education; the right to an adequate standard of living, including the rights to adequate food, housing, water and sanitation; and the right to the highest attainable standard of health. It also includes the right to work, rights at work, the right to take part in cultural life and the cultural rights of members of minorities and Indigenous Peoples.


enforced disappearance

The term enforced disappearance is used when there are reasonable grounds to believe that someone has been apprehended by the authorities or their agents, or people acting with their acquiescence, but the authorities refuse to acknowledge this or conceal the person’s fate or whereabouts, placing them outside the protection of the law.


excessive or unnecessary use of force

Excessive use of force means that which is excessive or disproportionate to the legitimate objective to be achieved or to the threat posed in a particular situation.


exoneration

See: deportation


extradition

The official process, generally regulated by treaty, whereby one country surrenders to another country an individual charged or convicted of a crime.


extrajudicial executions

Unlawful killings ordered by a government, or committed with its knowledge and support. They can be carried out by the military, police, or by civilians working with government forces. For example, Hamas forces in Gaza committed serious human rights abuses, including extrajudicial executions, torture and abductions, during the 2014 Gaza/Israel conflict.

See: enforced disappearances


F


fair trial

In both criminal and civil matters, courts must be independent and impartial. Everyone must be fully equal before the courts. With limited exceptions all hearings and judgments must be public.

Anyone accused of a crime has specific rights, including: to be informed promptly of the allegations against them; the presumption of innocence; adequate time, facilities, and means to prepare and present a defence; the assistance of independent legal counsel of their own choosing, including through confidential communications; to be present at their trial; to call relevant witnesses and to be able to cross-examine any witness against them; not to be compelled to testify against themselves or to confess guilt.


forced eviction

Forced eviction is the permanent or temporary removal against their will of individuals, families or communities from their homes or from land that they occupy, without the provision of, or access to, appropriate forms of legal or other protection.

Forced eviction has been recognized as a gross violation of a range of human rights, including the right to adequate housing. It is also often associated with other human rights violations including abuses of physical and mental integrity in the process of eviction and denial of the right to education as a result of eviction.


forcible repatriation

Normally refers to the return of a person against their will to their country of origin or habitual residence.

See: non-refoulement; refoulement; forcible return; deportation


forcible return

The return against one’s will from one state to another. Forcible return is a broader term than forcible repatriation as it does not necessarily have to be to the country of origin or habitual residence. For example, a person may be sent to a third country through which they have travelled.

See: deportation; forcible repatriation; non-refoulement; refoulement


freedom of expression

The right to seek, receive and share information and ideas, without fear or interference. It is closely linked with freedom of association (the right to meet with anyone you choose, for example, to form and join clubs, societies or trade unions) and the right to take part in a peaceful assembly, such as a demonstration or public meeting.


G


gender-based violence

Gender-based violence against women is violence directed against a woman because she is a woman or that affects women disproportionately. In other words, not all acts that harm a woman are gender-based. Men can suffer gender-based violence, for example, gay men who are attacked because they do not conform to socially approved views of masculinity. However, most gender-based violence is committed by men, and is directed against women and girls and linked to discrimination.


genocide

Genocide means any of the following acts when they are carried out “with intent to destroy, in whole or in part”, a national, ethnic, racial or religious group: killing or causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of a group, deliberately inflicting conditions of life on a group calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part, preventing births, or forcibly transferring children to another group.


H


hate speech

Hate speech is advocacy of national, racial or religious hatred that constitutes incitement to discrimination, hostility or violence. It should not be used as a synonym for implied or actual threats against individuals; it must have the two elements of advocacy of hatred and incitement.


human rights

All human rights are indivisible and universal. Avoid singling out a specific human right or group of rights to describe as fundamental or basic. If the intent is to point out that a particular right is non-derogable, explain what this means.

See: violations


human rights defenders

Human rights defenders are individuals who take non-violent actions to defend and protect human rights. While their own activities might focus on a specific area of human rights or on the human rights of a specific group, they must accept the universality of human rights as defined in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights; a person cannot claim to be a human rights defender if they deny some human rights or take any action that undermines the rights of others.

To determine whether a person is a human rights defender, the critical test is whether the person’s actions are based on concerns for human rights.

Human rights defenders can be journalists, lawyers, members of human rights organizations, trade unionists, health professionals or other service providers who fulfil essential medical or educational needs, or politicians who promote human rights and speak out against human rights violations. They may be friends or relatives of victims of human rights violations who stand by their loved ones despite threats and intimidation.


I


ill-treatment

See: cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment; torture


immediate release

Amnesty International calls for the immediate and unconditional release of prisoners of conscience. For other prisoners, we call for prompt and fair trial or release.

See: prisoner of conscience


immunity (from prosecution)

Allows an accused person to avoid prosecution for a crime because of their job or office. Amnesty International is opposed to immunity. For example, Amnesty has called for the rejection of immunity in the Central African Republic so that people who committed crimes during conflict there are brought to justice.


impunity

Impunity is when people who have committed crimes avoid punishment. For example, there is a ‘culture of impunity’ among police in the Philippines, who are able to torture people without fear of being punished. Amnesty calls for these people to be tried and sentenced fairly, according to the law.

See: immunity


incommunicado detention

When a detainee is denied any access to people outside the place of detention, in particular legal counsel, family members and an independent court.

Incommunicado detention is the context in which torture or other ill-treatment and enforced disappearances most frequently occur. If prolonged, it is in itself a form of torture or other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.

See: enforced disappearance; solitary confinement


internally displaced people

People forced to flee their homes because of armed conflict, generalized violence, human rights abuses or natural or human-made disasters, and who have sought safety elsewhere in the same country (they have not crossed an international border). Internally displaced people have the same rights as other citizens of their country.

See: displaced people; refugee


J


juvenile offender

In the context of the imposition of the death penalty, a juvenile offender is a person aged under 18 at the time the alleged capital offence was committed. Sentencing juvenile offenders to death is prohibited under international law.


M


migrant

A migrant moves around within their own country, or from one country to another, usually to find work, although there may be other reasons such as to join family. Some move voluntarily, while others are forced to leave because of economic hardship or other problems. People can migrate ‘regularly’, with legal permission to work and live in a country, or ‘irregularly’, without permission from the country they wish to live and work in.

Most international migrants live in Europe (72 million) followed by Asia (71 million) and North America (53 million).

See: asylum-seeker; refugee


moratorium

With regard to the death penalty, a public commitment made by the highest authorities, officially suspending either executions or the imposition and carrying out of all death sentences.


N


non-refoulement

The principle which obliges states not to return anyone to a country where he or she would risk serious human rights violations or abuses.

See: refugee; asylum-seeker; refoulement


P


prisoner of conscience

A prisoner of conscience is someone who is imprisoned because of who they are (sexual orientation, ethnic, national or social origin, language, birth, colour, sex or economic status) or their religious, political or other beliefs, and who hasn’t used or advocated violence. For example, Dr Mohammed al-Roken is a current prisoner of conscience in the United Arab Emirates, jailed for 10 years following a deeply unfair trial.


R


refoulement

Any measure (for example, expulsion, deportation, extradition, removal, rejection, interception) that forces an individual to return to a country where he or she would risk serious human rights violations or abuses. Refoulement is prohibited under international law.

See: asylum-seeker; non-refoulement; refugee


refugee

A refugee is a person who has fled their country of origin and is unable or unwilling to return because of a well-founded fear of being persecuted because of their race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion.

See: asylum-seeker; displaced people; internally displaced people; non-refoulement; refoulement


rendition

The transfer of people from one state to another or into the custody of another state by state agents or others working on behalf of a government, using means that bypass judicial and administrative due process.


S


“safe third country”

Term used to justify denying asylum-seekers access to a country or its asylum procedures because they are expected to seek protection in another (third) country, usually because they have spent time there or travelled through it after fleeing the country of persecution.


solitary confinement

Isolation of a prisoner or detainee from other prisoners, usually involving minimal contact with staff.

Depending on the length and other conditions of solitary confinement, or the reduced sensory stimulation that can result from solitary confinement, it may amount to cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment or to torture, and it may facilitate other ill-treatment and torture. Moreover, it can have serious detrimental effects on prisoners’ physical and mental health. Solitary confinement is not the same as incommunicado detention. A prisoner held in isolation from other prisoners may still have access to, for example, lawyers, family and independent medical care.

Amnesty International believes that no prisoner should be confined to prolonged solitary confinement and reduced sensory stimulation.

See: incommunicado detention


“sodomy”

Laws which criminalize people for having same-sex relations in private are sometimes referred to as “sodomy” or “buggery” laws.


summary trials, summary proceedings

Short trials or proceedings, often conducted without some or all of the normal procedural safeguards and therefore raising concerns about respect for fair trial rights.


summary executions

Executions carried out after speedy and grossly unfair trials or without any judicial proceedings at all.

See: extrajudicial executions


stay of execution

A stay of execution is a court order to temporarily suspend the execution of a court judgment or other court order.

In cases of death penalty, a stay of execution is often sought to defer the execution of the convicted person. This may occur if new evidence is discovered that will exonerate the convicted person or in attempts to have the sentence commuted. One example of a stay of execution in the death penalty context was the case of Anthony Haynes, when the US Supreme Court granted a stay of execution about two-and-a-half hours before he was scheduled to be put to death in Texas


T


torture

Torture is when someone in authority intentionally causes severe pain or suffering for a specific purpose. Such as to get information or a confession out of you, to punish, intimidate or threaten you.

It can be physical, such as beating, forcing into a painful position or sexual, such as rape. It can be psychological, such as sleep deprivation or public humiliation. Torture is illegal.

See: corporal punishment; cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.


U


unlawful detention

See: arbitrary detention


unlawful killings

Killings that are unlawful by international human rights or humanitarian law and standards, whether committed by or on the authority of security or armed forces under the control of a government or by armed groups. The term includes killings resulting from excessive use of force by law enforcement officials, and, in armed conflict, deliberate killings of civilians and other non-combatants by government forces or armed groups, and killings resulting from disproportionate or indiscriminate attacks.

See: extrajudicial executions


urgent action

Urgent Actions are a type of campaign used by Amnesty International to help and protect individuals suffering human rights violations.

This campaigning is based on a very simple idea: when someone is in immediate danger of serious human rights abuse, those responsible receive thousands of letters, faxes and telegrams from all over the world demanding action. This pressure has the potential to protect that person from further abuse. Since 1976 Amnesty International has issued over 25,000 Urgent Actions and mobilised over 150,000 people to stand up against injustice.


V


violations

As a general rule use the term human rights violations to refer to acts by state agents (violating the state’s obligations under international human rights law). This term can also be used to describe acts as experienced by victims. Armed groups, who have direct obligations under international humanitarian law in situations of armed conflict, can be described as committing violations of international humanitarian law. Otherwise, use the term human rights abuses to describe acts by armed groups and other non-state actors. Use the term abuses generally to cover acts by both state and non-state actors.


W


war crimes

War crimes are serious crimes committed during armed conflicts, which could include murdering prisoners of war, killing hostages, torture and destroying towns and villages. A recent example is in the Ukraine, where there is overwhelming evidence of ongoing war crimes, including torture and summary killings of prisoners.
